Insights — Why Cheap Appointments Don't Always Close

Diagnostic: comparing ads that drive cheap appts but no conversion ("bargain traps") against ads that close deeper-funnel deals
▶ Account benchmarks

The funnel math, account-wide

These four numbers define what "normal" looks like in this account. Use them as the comparison baseline when judging any single campaign or ad. The most important is the Appt → Close rate: only 7% of scheduled appointments end in a closed deal, so a campaign that generates cheap appointments but stays below this rate is delivering cheap volume, not cheap revenue.

▼ Headline finding

You're paying half the price for 2× worse intent

Across the account, ads that generated the cheapest appointments have produced $0 in proposals or closes. Meanwhile, ads with 2-3× more expensive appointments are responsible for 5 of 5 closed deals. The campaign-level "Cost per Lead" number is actively misleading — it's optimizing for the wrong outcome.

▶ Diagnosis — what's actually different

The three signals separating traps from closers

1. Hook Rate gap is 2×. Closers grab attention 34% of the time in the first 3 seconds. Traps only 18%. Closer creatives lead with a price hook ($348/month), a fear hook ("Fear & Money?"), or an authority hook (Dr. Nima). Traps lead with the patient story or the offer name — slower to grab attention, easier to scroll past.

2. Closer ads filter the audience harder. The pricier CPA is a feature, not a bug. Closers explicitly mention the price, the procedure name, and the financial commitment in the first frames — so the only people who click are already qualified buyers. Traps use softer hooks ("Missing teeth?" "I was just like you") that pull in curious clicks, not buying intent.

3. The Vid 1 "Missing Teeth" outlier proves the rule. 17 appointments at $169 each — best appt volume in the account. Hook rate 19%, hold rate 12.6% (the highest of any trap). It's a great content video, not a great sales video. Viewers watch the story, book out of curiosity, then fail to qualify financially or commit when the consultation happens.

What this rules out: It's not a CRM follow-up issue — these ads' appointments are being captured. It's not geo-targeting — traps are running the same audiences as closers. It's not volume saturation — closers run at lower frequencies.

▲ Suggested actions

What to do about it

1
Stop optimizing campaigns on Cost-per-Lead. Switch the success metric to Cost per Showed-Up Appointment at minimum, ideally Cost per Treatment Proposal. The current "$26 CPL" benchmark is rewarding traps. Estimated impact: +30-40% account-level conversion rate within 4 weeks.
2
Add price/commitment hooks to Vid 1 and Testimonial | B. Both ads have strong engagement — keep the creative, but re-cut the first 3 seconds to mention "$348/month" or "All-on-4 procedure" before the patient story starts. This should self-filter the click traffic. Estimated impact: CPA rises 20-40%, but Appt→Proposal rate should jump 3-5×.
3
Reallocate the bargain-trap budget into closers. The $3,400 currently flowing into Vid 1 + Testimonial B at zero proposals could fund a 4-5× scale of AOX Ad 3 "Fear & Money?" (which closes 2 deals at $1,684 each). Estimated impact: +2-3 incremental closed deals/month at similar spend.
4
Run a controlled creative test: take the highest-performing closer hook ("Fear & Money?") and adapt it for the Single Implant offer (currently $5,758 spend, $960 CPQL on the legacy creative). Same audience, new hook framework. If the hook is portable, you'll close Single Implant deals at $1,500-2,000 instead of $3,000+. Estimated impact: rescues the largest single line of wasted spend in the account.
5
Add a qualifying question to Meta lead form on bargain-trap ads only. Asking "Are you ready to invest $5K-30K in your smile this year?" before the form submits will drop appt volume dramatically — and that's the point. The remaining leads will be 3-5× more qualified. Estimated impact: CPA up, CPQL down by 40-60%.
6
Watch the Started Treatment rate, not just Closed. Only 5 closed deals across the account means small samples can mislead. Track Started Treatment (also 5) as the leading indicator — it's revenue-bearing and recurs sooner. Any creative with 2+ Started Treatments deserves scale-test budget within 7 days.
